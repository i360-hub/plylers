People apologize to me all the time for calling at the "wrong" time of year. Here's a secret from 24 years of [trimming trees](/tree-trimming) around Arkadelphia and Hot Springs: there's no wrong time to remove a dead or dangerous limb. That work happens the day you find it, any month of the year.

For everything else, timing genuinely matters. Trim at the right time and the tree heals fast and comes back stronger. Trim at the wrong time and you stress it, invite pests, or cut off next year's bloom. Here's how I schedule it.

## Late Winter Is King

If I could only trim trees one season a year, it would be February into early March. The trees are dormant, so cutting doesn't stress them. There are no leaves, so I can see the whole structure of the tree, every crossing limb and every weak fork. The wounds are open for the shortest time before spring growth seals them over. And the insects that find fresh cuts are still asleep.

That's when the big structural work should happen: shaping young trees, thinning heavy canopies, taking large limbs off mature oaks and pines. If you know a tree needs serious work, put it on the calendar for late winter and it will thank you.

## What Each Season Is Good For

**Spring.** Light work only. This is when trees are spending everything they have on new growth, so heavy cutting hits them hardest. Dead and broken limbs can always come out, but save the big cuts. One exception on the other side: trees that bloom in spring, like dogwoods and redbuds, get trimmed right after they finish flowering, so you don't cut off next year's show.

**Summer.** Fine for cleanup and correction. Summer is when you can see which limbs are actually dead, because they're the bare ones in a green canopy. It's also when storm damage gets handled, and around here that's most of my summer. Just keep heavy pruning off the list, especially in a drought year, because a stressed Arkansas tree in August doesn't need more problems.

**Fall.** The season everyone wants to trim, and honestly the trickiest one. Cuts made in fall heal slowly, because the tree is shutting down instead of growing. What fall is truly for is preparation: getting dead wood and weak limbs out before [ice season](/ice-storm-tree-damage-arkansas), and getting weight off anything hanging over the house. Think of fall trimming as storm-proofing, not shaping.

**Winter.** Back to the good stuff. Any time after the leaves drop, the dormant-season window is open.

## A Note on Oaks

Oaks get one extra rule. Fresh cuts on oaks in the warm months can attract the beetles that spread oak disease. It's a bigger problem in Texas than it is here, but I don't gamble with a 100-year-old [oak](/oak-tree-removal-arkadelphia-ar). Major oak work on my schedule happens in the dormant season, period. A dead limb still comes off whenever it shows up, but planned oak pruning waits for cold weather.

## The One Time Not to Cut

Never trim a tree that's under stress and pushing new growth right after damage. A tree that just lost half its canopy in a storm is running on stored energy, and every green limb left is keeping it alive. The instinct is to "clean it up" and even it out. Resist it. Take the broken and hanging pieces, then give the tree a season before deciding what else goes. Some of the trees I'm proudest of are ones a homeowner wanted to finish off, and a patient trim saved.

## How Often Trees Actually Need Trimming

Less often than the door-knockers tell you. A healthy mature shade tree does well on a look-over every year and a real trim every three to five years. Young trees are the exception, because a little shaping in the first ten years prevents the expensive problems at year forty. Fast growers like sweetgums and water oaks around [Hot Springs](/tree-service-hot-springs-ar) and the lake neighborhoods usually need attention more often than the slow, steady oaks.

If a crew knocks on your door after a storm offering to "top" your trees, close the door. Topping ruins trees, and no reputable service in this state does it.
