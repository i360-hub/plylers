Here's a question I get a few times a month, usually from folks who just moved here from somewhere with more rules: "Do I need a permit to take this tree down?"

The short answer for most homeowners in Arkansas: no. If the tree stands fully on your own property, you can usually take it down without asking anyone. Arkansas leans hard toward property rights. There's no statewide permit for cutting a tree in your own yard.

That said, "generally" is doing some work in that sentence. In 24 years of [removing trees](/tree-removal) around Arkadelphia, I've seen every one of the exceptions below trip somebody up. Here's the full picture.

## Where the Exceptions Live

**City rules.** Some Arkansas cities protect certain trees, mostly on commercial and development projects, not home yards. Fayetteville and Little Rock are the big examples. Small towns like ours mostly leave home removals alone, but rules change. For anything unusual, one call to city hall settles it. When I quote a job inside city limits, that check is part of my job, not yours.

**Street and public trees.** The tree between the sidewalk and the road is not yours to cut, even if it fills your gutters all fall. Public trees need the city's written OK, full stop. Cut one without it and a yard project turns into a fine.

**Trees on the property line.** A trunk that sits on the line belongs to you and your neighbor both, and it can't come down without their OK. Arkansas also has real penalties for cutting someone else's trees. "I thought it was on my side" won't save you. When a tree sits near a line, know where the line is before the saw starts.

**Waterfront trees.** State law limits cutting trees below the high-water mark on rivers and streams. If your place backs up to the Ouachita or the Caddo, the trees at the water's edge may not be yours to cut, even on your deed. Lake lots around DeGray add one more layer, since shoreline rules can apply near the water. On [lakefront jobs](/lakefront-tree-service-degray-lake-ar), checking that line comes first.

**Power line strips.** Trees inside a power line easement are mostly the utility's business. If a tree has grown into the lines, the call goes to Entergy before it goes to me.

## The Flip Side: When the City Can Make You Remove One

Here's the version of this question nobody asks until it happens. An Arkansas city can order you to remove a dead or dangerous tree. They send written notice and a deadline. If you don't act, the city can remove it and put the cost on your tax bill. I see this most with rentals and inherited houses where a dead tree stood too long.

The lesson is the one I give about [dying trees](/signs-tree-is-dying) anyway. A dead tree over a sidewalk or a neighbor's fence isn't just a risk. It can become a letter from the city. Handle it on your schedule, not theirs.

## What About HOAs and Historic Districts?

An HOA can have tree rules even where the city doesn't, and around the lake communities toward Hot Springs Village, they usually do. Approval forms, protected trees, rules about stumps you can see from the road. If you're under an HOA or POA, check the rulebook before you schedule. Same goes for houses in a historic district, where outside changes sometimes need review.

## The Practical Answer

For a normal tree in a normal Arkadelphia yard, you don't need a permit. You can stop worrying about it. For anything touching the list above, the check takes one phone call, and I make that call as part of the [estimate](/tree-removal-arkadelphia-ar) anyway. That's part of what you get from someone who's worked here since 2002. I already know which lots back up to a right-of-way, which streets the city cares about, and which lake lots have shoreline rules.

Don't let the permit question stop you from dealing with a dangerous tree. It almost never applies. When it does, it's paperwork, not a wall.
