Every summer I get the same call, usually in August. "My pine was green a month ago and now the whole top is brown. What happened?" Nine times out of ten, the answer is bark beetles, and by the time you can see the damage from the yard, the fight for that tree is usually over.

Clark County is pine country. Loblolly and shortleaf pines are what we grow, what we log, and what I spend most of my year [taking down](/pine-tree-removal-arkadelphia-ar). Bark beetles are the biggest reason healthy-looking pines die fast around here, so it's worth knowing what to look for before it costs you a tree, or three.

## What Bark Beetles Actually Do

Pine bark beetles are small insects, each one about the size of a grain of rice. The two you'll hear about in Arkansas are Ips beetles and the southern pine beetle. Both do the same basic damage. The adults bore through the bark and tunnel through the layer just underneath it, which is the part of the tree that moves water and food. Once that layer is cut off all the way around the trunk, the tree starves, no matter how green it still looks today.

The beetles also carry a fungus that stains the wood blue and plugs up the tree's plumbing even faster. That's why a beetle-hit pine can go from green to brown in a matter of weeks.

## The Signs, in the Order You'll See Them

**Pitch tubes on the bark.** Small blobs of sap, white to reddish, about the size of a piece of popcorn, dotted up the trunk. That's the tree trying to push the beetles back out. That's the earliest sign you'll get, and it's also the easiest one to miss.

**Sawdust in the bark crevices.** Fine, reddish-brown dust collecting in the bark and around the base of the tree, like someone sprinkled cinnamon on it.

**Fading crown.** The needles at the top go from green to a dull olive, then yellow, then rusty red. Once the whole crown turns, the tree is already dead. It just doesn't know it yet.

**Bark falling away.** Woodpeckers show up to eat the larvae and knock the bark loose. If you peel a flake off and see winding trails carved on the inside, that's the beetle galleries, and the story is over for that tree.

## Why It Spreads to Your Other Pines

Here's the part that costs people money. Beetles don't hit one tree and leave. A dying pine puts out a scent that pulls more beetles in, and southern pine beetles in particular will move to the neighboring trees and keep going. I've watched one ignored pine turn into a dead patch of six or eight in a single Arkansas summer.

Drought makes it worse. Stressed pines can't produce enough sap to push the beetles out, so a dry summer like we get in this part of the state turns the whole yard into easy pickings. Trees damaged by storms or construction are on the same list. If you had dirt work done near your pines in the last year or two, watch them close.

## When Beetles Hit, and Why Fresh-Cut Land Draws Them

Beetle season in Arkansas runs from spring green-up through the first hard frost, and they raise several generations in one year. That's why a yard can look fine in June and have three dead pines by Labor Day. The worst years follow dry summers, when every pine in the county is too stressed to defend itself.

One more thing folks around Gurdon and Amity learn the hard way: fresh-cut timber pulls beetles in. Ips beetles breed in logging slash, stumps, and downed tops, then move to the standing pines next door. If the land beside you was logged this year, or you cleared part of your own place, keep a close eye on the pines you kept. The same goes for lots cleared for a new build. I see beetle hits on the leave-trees of construction sites more than anywhere else.

## Can the Tree Be Saved?

I'll be straight with you: usually not. Once the crown is fading or the bark is sliding off, no spray and no watering schedule brings it back. The realistic goal is protecting the pines that haven't been hit yet.

That means getting the infested tree down and off the property fast. A dead beetle tree standing in your yard is a beetle nursery, raising the next generation that moves to your healthy trees. It's also a [dying tree](/signs-tree-is-dying) that gets more brittle and more dangerous to remove every month it stands. Beetle-killed pines rot from the inside quicker than most people expect, and a rotten pine near a house is not a tree I want to climb a year from now.

## What to Do If You See the Signs

First, don't stack the wood next to your healthy pines. Beetles ride along in cut logs, so hauling the wood off matters as much as cutting the tree.

Second, get someone who knows pines to look at the rest of them. Sometimes the neighbors of a hit tree can be protected if you move quick. When I do a [removal in Arkadelphia](/tree-removal-arkadelphia-ar) or anywhere in Clark County, I walk the other pines on the property while I'm there and tell you what I see. That look is free and it's saved more than a few trees.

If a beetle-killed pine is leaning toward your house or already dropping limbs, treat it like the [emergency](/emergency-tree-service-arkadelphia-ar) it is. Dead pines don't give much warning when they let go.
